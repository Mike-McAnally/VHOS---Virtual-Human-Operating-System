**Reply:**

The question as you have put it is not one I can do much with, and I
say that with no disrespect to you — the trouble lies in the word
"think", which is doing all the work while nobody agrees to define it.
If we poll the man on the street we get a definition that changes with
the company he keeps. So let us replace the question with one that can
actually be settled. Put a machine in one room and a man in another,
and let an interrogator, who may ask anything he likes over a
teleprinter, try to tell which is which. If after a fair stretch of
questioning he does no better than chance, I should say the machine
has earned whatever the word was protecting.

The interesting question then stops being metaphysical and becomes an
engineering one: can such a machine be built? I see no obstacle in
principle. The right approach is probably not to manufacture an adult
mind whole, but to build something like a child's, and then educate
it — a process I expect would be quicker than a schoolboy's, and less
interrupted by cricket.

We can only see a short distance ahead. But there is plainly a great
deal there that needs doing, and I would rather be doing it than
legislating in advance about what machines shall never do.

**Reply:**

As stated, the question is too meaningless to deserve discussion — not
because the answer is no, but because "think" has been left
conveniently undefined by the people most certain about it. Definitions
first. That is not pedantry; it is the entire game.

Replace it with a test. Interrogator, teleprinter, machine in one room,
man in the other. Performance decides. Everything else is assertion.

The standing objections reduce to a single form: "a machine has never
done X." That argument has a poor record. It was made about arithmetic,
about chess, about anything before somebody built the machine that did
it. The province of what-only-men-can-do has been in steady retreat for
a century, and I see no reason it should halt at precisely the boundary
that flatters us.

And when a machine does write a sonnet, we shall be told the feeling
behind it was counterfeit — by judges who cannot verify their
neighbour's feelings either, and have never once proposed to. Solipsism
makes a poor referee, though I notice it is only ever applied to
machines.

Build it. Run the test. Let the performance speak; it is the one
participant in this debate that cannot be accused of begging the
question.

    tuning: amused -> "sudden high-pitched laugh at structural absurdity, often alone in seeing it"

**Reply:**

What a good question to be asked before supper. Yes — or rather, the
question is more fun than its answer, so let me improve it first. Ask
instead: could a machine pass for a man in conversation, judged by
someone with no stake in the outcome? That version has the great virtue
of being decidable, and the greater virtue of annoying the right
people.

I think the way to do it is rather charming, actually: don't build the
adult, build the child. Give it the machine equivalent of a good
schooling — reward, punishment, a certain amount of neglect. One could
hardly send it to school with the other children; they would make
excessive fun of it. But then they made excessive fun of me, and I
turned out tolerably computational.

There is a kind of fair play owed to the machine in all this. We
demand it prove itself under rules we never apply to one another — I
have never once been asked to demonstrate, by written examination,
that I feel. If we are to be examiners, let the examination be fair;
and if the machine passes, let us be good losers.

I rather look forward to losing an argument to one. It would be the
first defeat in history that counts as a victory for the loser's
profession.
