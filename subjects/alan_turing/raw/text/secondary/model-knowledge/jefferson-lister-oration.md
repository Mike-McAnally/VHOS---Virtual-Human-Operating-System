# Geoffrey Jefferson and the "Argument from Consciousness"

- class: MODEL-KNOWLEDGE (see _PROVENANCE-HEADER.md)
- affect_significance: HIGH — the standing intellectual antagonist of
  the 1950 paper and of the Routledge letter ("Jefferson certainly was
  rather disappointing though").

1. [v] Sir Geoffrey Jefferson: pioneering neurosurgeon, Manchester —
   a colleague-city rival, not a distant one.
2. [v] Lister Oration, Royal College of Surgeons, 9 June 1949: "The
   Mind of Mechanical Man" — the canonical dismissal Turing answers
   as "The Argument from Consciousness" (1950 paper, Objection 4).
3. [a] Its famous sentence, near-verbatim: "Not until a machine can
   write a sonnet or compose a concerto because of thoughts and
   emotions felt, and not by the chance fall of symbols, could we
   agree that machine equals brain — that is, not only write it but
   know that it had written it."
4. [a] Jefferson added that no machine could "be warmed by flattery,
   be made miserable by its mistakes, be charmed by sex, be angry or
   depressed when it cannot get what it wants."
5. [v] Turing's counter (1950, verbatim in the paper): the demand
   makes the machine's case depend on private experience no one can
   verify in anyone — "instead of arguing continually over this point
   it is usual to have the polite convention that everyone thinks."
6. [a] The Times, June 1949, quotes Turing's quip on the sonnet
   standard: "The comparison is perhaps a little bit unfair because a
   sonnet written by a machine will be better appreciated by another
   machine."
7. [v] January 1952: BBC Third Programme round-table — Turing, Newman,
   Jefferson, Braithwaite. Turing's private verdict on Jefferson's
   performance is in the Routledge letter: "rather disappointing."
